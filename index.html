How to read this

“Feels like” is the apparent temperature (Steadman): air temperature adjusted for humidity and wind, because humid air slows the body's cooling by sweat. We use the shade formulation, which has no sunshine term and is therefore the right one for overnight. On humid nights it reads a little above the air temperature; in dry, breezy air a little below.

Why apparent temperature and not WBGT or wet-bulb? A review of the heat-mortality literature finds that in temperate Europe, plain air temperature predicts heat deaths about as well as any humidity index, and where a humidity-inclusive index adds anything it is apparent temperature. WBGT is built for daytime physical exertion in the sun and is not the validated choice for overnight sleep or for European mortality.

An overnight window runs 21:00 to 09:00 the next morning, in each city's local time. Each night is labeled by its evening date.

Two different questions

SLEEP Will it be an uncomfortable, restless night? Sleep degrades continuously as nights get warmer — there is no magic cut-off. As rough benchmarks, a feels-like that stays above 20° all night means a warm, restless night for people without air-conditioning; above 25° all night is oppressive, with very poor sleep. That is what the per-night table and the hours-above columns describe.

VULNERABLE Are we worried about people dying? Heat deaths in older people are driven less by any single hot night than by consecutive nights where the body never gets a cool recovery window. The 2003 Paris heatwave study found multi-day minimum (night-time) temperature — not daytime heat — predicted elderly deaths, and a 2025 study across 34 European countries found back-to-back day-and-night “compound” heat carried more than twice the mortality risk of daytime-only heat. So we flag runs of nights with no overnight relief (feels-like never dropping below 20°), which matters most for the elderly, the unwell, and other vulnerable groups.

Uncertainty: the “hours ≥ threshold” columns show the median across the 40-member ICON weather ensemble, with the 10th–90th percentile range in brackets — e.g. “4 h (2–6)” means most ensemble members expect 2 to 6 hours at or above the threshold.

Important caveat: we forecast outdoor temperature, but what affects sleep and health is the temperature in the bedroom. Homes without air-conditioning often stay warmer than the outside air overnight and cool more slowly, so indoor conditions can be worse than these numbers suggest.
